The ethical issue I'm exploring is the human touch that nurses have when "doing their rounds". As part of delivering medication to patients, it's also a chance for nurses to have a semi-regular check in with patients, ensuring that any issues haven't progressed when the patient was alone, often done via dynamic observation and discussion. While a robot can have sensors to replicate some of the observational part of this, it's reasonable to assume that most patients, especially elder ones, are unlikely to start a conversation with a robot. Nurses also are usually willing to do the smaller things to make a patient's life easier in the moment, such as filling up water or sourcing extra blankets, both things a robot's AI may not be trained to adjust to in the moment,

There are two ways this can be mitigated. The first is by taking these factors into account when training and designing the robot. Patients are unlikely to want to "chat" with robots designed solely as pill dispensers, but more humanoid designs are likely to be more appealing. This is something that's been explored in Japan in other service roles already, and more adaptable robots are coming from companies such as Tesla. Secondly, a robot's AI should be trained to understand and adapt to other requests while still being able to perform their tasks, which will require a tight balancing of priorities.
