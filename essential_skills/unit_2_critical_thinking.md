### Lesson 1 - Critical Thinking
- Analysis - Breaking down info into it's constituent parts to understand it's meaning
- Evaluation - Assessing the credibility, accuracy, and validity of information and arguments
- Interpretation - Understanding the significance and implications of information and ideas
- Inferrence - Drawing logical conclusions and making reasoned judgements based on evidence
- Explanation - Clearly and coherently articulating reasoning and conclusions
- Self-regulation - reflecting and monitoring ones own biases and assumptions

critical thinking for problem solving
    - gather relevant info
    - evaluate potential solutions
    - making informed decisions based on evidence and logical reasoning

Critical - being thoughtful, asking questions about what you are reading, don't take anything
at face value.
    - ask questions about the strenghs and weaknesses of an argument or idea
    - analyse and evaluate informatuion/theories/practices
    - be aware of yours and others biases

Blooms Taxonomy
- Knowledge
- Understanding
    - "what facts or ideas show...", "which statements support..."
- Application
    - "what would result if...", "what facts would you select to show..."
- Analysis
    - draw connections between ideas and concepts
    - Answer questions like "why", "how", "what if"
- Synthesis
    - Use given facts/connections to create new theories
    - synthesise information in a different way
    - "what changes would you make to solve...", "how would you improve..."
- Evaluation
    - Judge, assess, criticise information
    - Make a decision to support your work
        - Produce new or original work
    - "So what...", "What's next..."

Try to avoid confirmation bias

Critically thinking about a text
1 - get a description
    - the 5 Ws
2 - Analysis
    - How, Why, What if
    - EX: How was the research conducted, why are these theories discussed
    - Does the data presented actually support their conclusion
3 - Evaluation
    - " So What", "Whats next"
    - "How relevant is this text, what is your position, how does this relate to other information"
    - Evaluate types of evidence
        - empirical, statistical, expert testimony, qualitative data, historical records,
        - case studies, theoretical models

### Lesson 2 - Critical Reading
* Active Engagement - summarise the main ideas into your own words to ensure you understand the
meaning of the words. Use reading strategies instead of the whole text, and note taking
techniques to record the important details
* Analysis - What does it mean? Identify the argument of a text, examine the structure and how
it supports the authors purpose.
* Interpretation - Understand the context, identify biases.
* Evaluation of argument - Look at the evidence provided, compare/constrast with other similar
works, examine assumptions of the argument and consider alternative perspectives.
* Reflelction on text - Connect your prior knowledge and relate it to the content. For your
own opinion based on critical analysis on the text
* Synthesis - integrate information and draw conclusions or create a new argument.

Critical reading is:
* only reading relevant sections
* making connections between sources
* knowing why you're reading something
* evaluating arguments and evidence
* only making notes on relevant info
* exploring different viewpoints

Suitability of a source: relevance, recency, is it academic?

Effective reading strategies:
* Skimming
* Scanning - looking for specific information and ignoring the rest of the text
* Close reading
* section hopping

### Lesson 3 - Critical Writing
* Analysis - breaking down complex information into smaller parts to understand how they work 
            together, examine the structure of an argument, the evidence provided, the 
            assumptions under the claims
* Evaluation - Assess the validity, relevance, significance of the information. Question the
            credibility, the strength of evidence and the logic of conclusions
* Argumentation - Present your own reasoned arguments supported with evidence. State a clear 
                position, back it up with reasoning and evidence, and address counterargument
* Clarity and Precision - Writing clearly and precisely to convey your analysis and evaluation  
                        effectively, avoid vague language, be specific about points of evidence
* Structure - Organise your writing in a logical and coherent way. This involves an introduction,
                a body that develops an analysis and evaluation, and a conclusion that 
                summarises your findings
* Engagement with sources - Critically engaging with a range of sources to support your argument,
                            not just summarising, but analysing and critiquing in the context
                            of your argument


Writing that goes beyond mere description   
    - analysis, evaluation, synthesis of information
    - Use higher quality academic resources, not just wikipedia/blog posts etc
    - present facts and interpret them. What do these mean?
    - organised around a central argument
